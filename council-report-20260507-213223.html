LLM Council Report
Should MassageMap's homepage have a search bar only, or also show featured therapist cards below the fold?
Advisor Positions
Contrarian
No cards — search-only is the confidence statement
First Principles
Wrong question — fix search clarity first
Expansionist
Cards — social proof + revenue upside
Outsider
Cards — but editorial, not algorithmic
Executor
Not yet — copy fix first, add schema field now
Chairman's Verdict

Where the Council Agrees

The province grid alone is a weak trust signal. The Contrarian, Outsider, and Executor all arrive at this independently — the current structure doesn't answer the first-time visitor's question: "Are there real, vetted people on here?" A form asking users to self-sort into nine provinces before the product earns trust is a filing cabinet, not a welcome.

Early-stage supplier density is the critical constraint on featured cards. Every advisor who engaged with implementation flagged this. Cards with thin or geographically mismatched coverage destroy trust faster than no cards. This is the landmine the Expansionist glosses over.

Copy is underused and undervalued. The current sub-line ("Browse therapists and spas across South Africa") does no trust work. This is the lowest-effort, highest-certainty improvement available right now.

Where the Council Clashes

Search-only as confidence vs. search-only as coldness. The Contrarian and First Principles argue that a clean search interface is a confidence statement. The Outsider and Expansionist argue that at 375px, one viewport of form inputs gives the visitor nothing to feel. Both are right about different users — the disagreement is about what "done well" requires, and whether you have the suppliers to support it today.

Revenue potential of featured placement. The Expansionist frames it as a natural subscription upsell. The Contrarian frames paid placement as exactly the classifieds failure mode. Both are correct — paid featured cards are legitimate at maturity and a trap at early stage. The disagreement is about timing, not principle.

Blind Spots the Council Caught

Location detection — the thing that dissolves the entire debate. All five advisors accepted the province grid as a fixed given. None questioned whether province-first is the right entry point for a mobile user in transit. Three peer reviewers flagged this independently: if the homepage's first action is "detect your location," you show real nearby supply, or fall back to the province grid on denial. The featured-cards question becomes moot.

The three-sided product dynamic. All five advisors reasoned exclusively from the client side. Early-stage suppliers deciding whether to register will land on the homepage and ask "does this directory look worth paying for?" Featured cards answer that question for suppliers as much as customers.

The returning user. Four reviewers flagged this. Every advisor treated the homepage as a first-visit problem. Directory stickiness depends on repeat searchers. A search-only homepage has no memory and no shortcut for the returning visitor.

The Recommendation

Do not build featured cards yet. Add location detection as the primary CTA. Fix the copy today.

The Contrarian wins the featured-cards argument on implementation risk — but only because the Expansionist's assumption (good photos, right locations, suppliers ready to feature) cannot be guaranteed at early stage. The moment that assumption is validated — six or more active, photographed, verified suppliers in a single area — the Outsider's case becomes correct and featured cards should be added.

The location detection blind spot is the real answer the council missed. The province grid is the right fallback, but the primary CTA on a mobile-first directory in 2026 should be "Find therapists near me." This collapses the search path from three taps (province → town → suburb) to one. Add featured: true to the supplier schema now so the capability exists when density justifies it.

The One Thing to Do First

Add a "Find therapists near me" button as the primary CTA above the province grid — GPS detection on tap, graceful fallback to the province grid on denial — and update the sub-line copy to explicitly name vetting and exclude adult services.

Advisor Responses +
The Contrarian

The featured-cards idea has a landmine baked into it: supplier count per area may be low. What does the homepage look like when there are three therapists in Johannesburg and none in Polokwane? You either show the same three cards to everyone regardless of location (dishonest, breaks trust) or empty slots (dead directory). Neither survives contact with a skeptical first-time visitor.

"Featured" implies curation. Who decides? Paid placement recreates classifieds. Algorithmic surfaces whoever registered first. Manual admin doesn't scale past 50 suppliers.

Adult directories use exactly the grid-of-faces pattern. Cards without strong vetting markers accelerate the wrong association. Search-only isn't sparse — it's a confidence statement. Featured cards are a patch on weak search UX, not a design decision.

The First Principles Thinker

The actual question is: what job does the homepage do? Users have an immediate specific need — a massage, nearby, now or soon. The homepage's job is to eliminate uncertainty about whether this is the right place, then get out of the way.

Early-stage supply is the real constraint. Featured cards with thin coverage reveal directory immaturity. A Johannesburg user who sees three cards, two of which are in Pretoria, has already lost trust. The trust signal needed is clarity of purpose, not visual richness.

The wrong question is "search-only vs. featured cards." The right question: what one thing does a first-time visitor need to believe to start their search? Answer that in copy and visual hierarchy, then build the search path.

The Expansionist

Featured cards — but framing is everything. A well-composed card — verified badge, real photo, professional name, suburb — tells the visitor "these people were checked." That's the opposite of clutter. That's what Gumtree cannot do.

"Featured" can become a revenue line. Paid homepage visibility is a natural subscription upsell. Five beautifully presented vetted therapists beats a province grid that leads to empty results. Scarcity, when honest, reads as selectiveness.

Cards make the homepage solve a trust problem AND a discovery problem simultaneously. Clients in transit who don't know which suburb they want get exposed to supply they wouldn't have found through the drill-down. The province grid stays. Search stays. But below it, three or four cards that say "this is what vetted looks like" — that's the homepage doing double duty.

The Outsider

The province grid is a filing cabinet. Strangers don't open filing cabinets — they read the room first. At 375px, province selector plus search bar is the entire viewport. Nothing to feel. No signal that real humans are behind this.

The Gumtree risk comes from the wrong KIND of cards — price tags, low-quality photos, zero curation signal. A card with a professional headshot, vetted badge, named suburb says "these people were checked."

Solve the early-stage problem editorially: hand-pick 4-5 excellent profiles as permanent featured cards. Call the section "Featured Therapists" not "Near You." One viewport of search inputs cannot answer the question "are there real, vetted people on here?" A single well-composed therapist card can.

The Executor

The current homepage already has a hero headline and sub-line plus the province grid. The structure is correct. The trust work to do today is copy-level, not architecture-level.

Change the sub-line to "Every listing personally vetted. No adult services." That single line, costing five minutes, does more trust work than a featured-cards section requiring a week of engineering.

Do not build featured cards until supplier density justifies it. Add featured: true to the supplier schema now so the field exists when needed. Move on.
